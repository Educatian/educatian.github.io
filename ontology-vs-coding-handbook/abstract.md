# Ontology and Qualitative Coding Schemes — An Epistemological Bridge

A practical onboarding handbook for graduate AI researchers who are fluent in
knowledge graphs, OWL/RDF, embeddings, and prompt engineering, but new to
interpretive qualitative methods such as grounded theory and reflexive thematic
analysis. The guide refuses the common flattening that treats a coding scheme
as just an annotation taxonomy. Instead it shows that formal ontology and
qualitative coding scheme are two distinct ways of organizing meaning that sit
at different layers of the meaning stack: ontology answers *what kinds of things
exist and what relations hold among them* with logical commitment, while a
coding scheme answers *how do these informants make sense of this phenomenon*
with interpretive warrant. Across ten sections the handbook gives honest
definitions, names the shared DNA, draws the epistemological split (closed-
versus open-world, formal versus interpretive, normative versus emergent,
realism versus reflexivity), surveys validation procedures, lists six
misconceptions AI researchers commonly carry into qualitative work, and offers
a decision tree plus three worked examples (a knowledge-graph case, a
thematic-analysis case, and a hybrid discourse-network case). It treats the
LLM-as-coder literature as a live front rather than settled fact, flags four
ongoing controversies, and closes with a glossary and references drawn from a
verified 28-source literature scan. Author: Dr. Jewoong Moon, ADDIE Lab,
University of Alabama. Version 0.1, 2026-05-07. CC BY 4.0.
